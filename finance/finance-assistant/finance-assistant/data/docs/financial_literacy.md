# Financial Literacy Fundamentals

## Understanding Net Worth

Net worth is the most important financial health metric. It is calculated as:

**Net Worth = Total Assets − Total Liabilities**

Assets: cash, savings, investments, property, vehicle value.
Liabilities: loans, credit card balances, mortgage balance.

A positive and growing net worth means you are building wealth, even if monthly cash flow feels tight.

## Good Debt vs Bad Debt

Not all debt is equal.

**Good debt** finances something that grows in value or increases your earning power:
- Education loans (when the career ROI is clear)
- Business loans with positive returns
- Mortgage (if property appreciates)

**Bad debt** finances depreciating assets or pure consumption:
- High-interest personal loans for lifestyle expenses
- Credit card balances carried month to month
- Buy-now-pay-later for discretionary purchases

Priority: Always pay off high-interest bad debt before investing.

## Understanding Compound Interest

Compound interest is earning interest on your interest. It is the engine of wealth building and debt destruction — it works for you in savings and against you in debt.

Example: Rs. 10,000 saved at 10% annual return:
- After 10 years: ~Rs. 25,937
- After 20 years: ~Rs. 67,275
- After 30 years: ~Rs. 174,494

Starting early matters more than the amount. A 25-year-old saving Rs. 5,000/month will typically retire with more than a 35-year-old saving Rs. 10,000/month.

## Inflation and Purchasing Power

Inflation erodes the value of money sitting in low-interest accounts. If inflation is 6% and your savings account pays 3%, your purchasing power is shrinking at 3% per year.

To beat inflation, money must be invested in assets that grow faster than the inflation rate — stocks, real estate, or inflation-linked instruments.

## Diversification

Never put all savings into a single asset or account. Diversification reduces risk without proportionally reducing returns.

Simple diversification for beginners:
- Emergency fund in a savings account
- Medium-term goals in fixed deposits or bonds
- Long-term wealth in equity index funds

## Understanding Credit Scores

A credit score reflects your borrowing history and reliability. A higher score means:
- Lower interest rates on loans
- Easier approval for housing, car loans, credit cards

How to improve your score:
- Pay all bills on time, every time
- Keep credit utilization below 30%
- Do not apply for multiple credit products simultaneously
- Maintain old credit accounts (history length matters)

## The Importance of Insurance

Insurance is not an investment — it is risk management. Without adequate insurance, a single medical emergency or accident can erase years of savings.

Essential insurance types:
- **Health insurance**: covers hospitalisation and medical costs
- **Life insurance**: protects dependants if you die prematurely (term insurance is the most cost-effective)
- **Vehicle insurance**: legally required and protects against accident costs

## Setting Financial Goals

Effective financial goals are SMART:
- **Specific**: "Save Rs. 500,000 for house down payment"
- **Measurable**: track monthly progress
- **Achievable**: aligned with current income
- **Relevant**: matches your values and life plans
- **Time-bound**: "Within 3 years"

Separate goals into short-term (under 1 year), medium-term (1–5 years), and long-term (5+ years). Each category needs a different savings or investment strategy.
